Matcha: The Flavor of Tradition

Exploring the uniqueness and intricacies of matcha through its different cultivation and production processes

The Difference of Matcha

The cultivation and production make matcha different from other teas.

First of all, only the newest spring buds and young tea leaves of the shade grown plants are hand-picked for matcha production. The window for production is very limited, which is one of the reasons why this green powder is one of the most expensive teas.

Second, instead of being rolled, shaped, and dried like traditional green tea leaves, the leaves destined for matcha are laid flat to dry, then stripped of their stems and veins. The remaining leaf material is ground in slow-turning stone mills, yielding a smooth green tea powder.

Third, traditional tea leaves are steeped in hot water to extract of the flavor and chemical properties from the tea leaves into a brewed tea that is sipped. Matcha green tea powder, however, is whisked into hot water, suspended in the water, is consumed with each sip

Finally, matcha powder production and cultivation is not just a delicate task, but also an art of its own. Because of that, tea producers are still continuing practicing these unique traditions up to this day. This will help you understand why matcha is more popular than any other green teas.
